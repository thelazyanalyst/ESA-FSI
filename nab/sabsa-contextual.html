Contextual Architecture — The Business View

The Contextual Architecture is the top layer of the SABSA framework. It captures the business context — who NAB is, what it is trying to achieve, what risks it faces, and the environmental constraints it operates within. Everything in the layers below must be traceable back to this layer. This document is derived from NAB's public annual results, CEO statements, investor presentations, and cybersecurity disclosures (2023–2026).

WHAT — Business Assets
Goals · Values · Asset Taxonomy · Success Factors · Targets
Business Goals & Decisions

Extend NAB's position as Australia's leading business bank. Grow business lending, drive deposit growth, and strengthen proprietary home lending. Deliver customer-centricity through digital, data, and AI. Execute ambitious social commitments: A$60B in housing finance by 2030 and A$1B in First Nations business lending by end 2026. Transform technology infrastructure toward cloud-native while reducing complexity through AI-augmented processes.

Business Values
  • Business bank leadership — identity built around serving SMEs, commercial, and agribusiness customers; Australia's leading business bank is NAB's core strategic claim
  • "Secure by Design" — security as a design principle, not a control layer; "shift left" philosophy embeds security into development from inception
  • Customer-centricity via AI — "Digital, data and AI are critical enablers for the delivery of our strategic ambition of customer-centricity" (CEO Andrew Irvine)
  • Social impact — A$60B housing commitment; A$1B First Nations lending; specialist support for agri, health, and professional services communities
  • SME empowerment — free CrowdStrike security for SMB banking customers (12 months); positions NAB as a security partner, not just a lender
Taxonomy of Business Assets
  • Business & Private Banking franchise — NAB's dominant division; A$1.74B cash earnings (1H25); Australia's leading SME and commercial bank by reputation and lending market share
  • Lending book — gross loans +5.9% YoY (FY25); business lending +9% FY25; deposits fund 84% of total lending — strong liability base
  • ubank (digital subsidiary) — digital-only consumer bank; passkeys deployed; passwords phased out within 5 years; consumer banking growth engine
  • BNZ (Bank of New Zealand) — wholly-owned NZ subsidiary; NZD $1,499M statutory net profit FY25; total lending +4.6%; deposits +5.8%
  • Cloud and AI platform — 84% of on-premises infrastructure migrated to public multi-cloud (AWS + Azure); Amazon Q Developer deployed to 1,000 developers (18% productivity uplift; 40% production code from AI suggestions)
  • "Customer brain" — centralised customer intelligence layer across all touchpoints; enables personalised engagement and cross-sell at scale
  • Fusion Centre — integrated physical security, cybersecurity, and global operations; faster incident detection and response than separate function model
  • Capital and balance sheet — CET1 11.70% (FY25); deposits fund 84% of lending; collective provisions / credit RWA 1.33%; IRB-accredited by APRA
Critical Success Factors
  • Business bank growth execution — sustaining +9% business lending growth and extending the leading market position is NAB's defining strategic test
  • Proprietary home lending channel — growing proprietary channel share from 38% (FY24) to 41% (FY25) and beyond reduces broker dependency and margin dilution
  • Cloud transformation completion — 84% migrated; Technology Modernisation Score reducing ~13%/year; must complete to unlock full AI and automation benefits
  • AI productivity realisation — Amazon Q Developer, trust deed AI, and GenAI campaigns must generate measurable cost and revenue outcomes to justify ongoing investment
  • Deposit growth maintenance — 84% deposit-funded lending is a strength; must retain deposit market share as RBA rate cycle evolves
  • Cybersecurity posture — Fusion Centre and CrowdStrike partnership are differentiators; a material security incident would undermine the "Secure by Design" brand claim
Business Targets
  • Business lending: continue above-market growth; A$128B in new business lending supported in 2025; +9% YoY FY25
  • Housing: A$60B in housing finance by 2030 (~55,000 first home buyers, 50,000 new homes)
  • First Nations: A$1B in First Nations business lending by end 2026
  • Proprietary home lending: grow channel share above 41% (FY25 level); target proprietary drawdowns as proportion of total
  • NIM: 1.74% (FY25, up 3bps); defend through deposit repricing and business lending volume
  • Capital: CET1 target >11.25%; FY25 actual 11.70%; IRB capital efficiency maintained
  • AI productivity: sustain 18%+ developer productivity via Amazon Q; expand GenAI initiatives across business banking and customer engagement

WHY — Business Motivation
Risk · Opportunities & Threats · Risk Management Strategy
Opportunities & Threats
Opportunities
  • Business lending momentum — +9% FY25; A$128B new lending in 2025; SME market share growth as competitors retrench from business banking complexity
  • CrowdStrike SMB differentiator — free enterprise-grade security for SMB customers creates loyalty and cross-sell opportunity beyond lending
  • Amazon Q Developer at scale — 18% productivity uplift across 1,000 developers; 40% production code from AI suggestions; technology modernisation accelerates
  • First Nations and specialist sector banking — A$1B lending commitment creates new market segment relationships in agri, health, and Indigenous communities
  • ubank digital growth — passkeys deployed; passwordless banking ahead of competitors; attracts younger, digitally-native consumer segment
Threats
  • Personal banking margin compression — Personal Banking down 9.2% (1H25); home loan competition from non-banks continues to pressure retail margins
  • CIB pressure — Corporate & Institutional Banking down 7.6% (1H25); fee income and margins under pressure in institutional segment
  • Cyber threats to Fusion Centre model — physical-cyber integration creates a high-value target; integrated centre failure would disable multiple security functions simultaneously
  • AI risk in credit decisioning — "Customer brain" and GenAI models introduce model risk and algorithmic bias risk at scale; APRA scrutiny of AI governance increasing
  • Weak productivity as macro risk — CEO (2025 AGM) cited weak Australian productivity as a structural economic risk; reduces SME credit demand growth
  • Talent competition for AI specialists — Pete Steel (Group Exec, Digital, Data & AI) recruited from Lloyds; competition for AI talent is global and intensifying
Risk Management Strategy
  • "Secure by Design" — five-pillar framework (Protect; Security Culture; Simplification & Automation; Layered Security; Secure by Design); led by CSO Sandro Bucchianeri; security shifts left into the development lifecycle
  • Fusion Centre model — physical security, cybersecurity, and global operations integrated for faster incident response; reduces mean time to detect and respond
  • CrowdStrike enterprise endpoint security — NAB enterprise protection extended to SMB banking customers (12 months free); reduces third-party risk from SME partner ecosystem
  • Graph databases for financial crime — fund flow monitoring and AML/fraud detection using graph analytics; detects complex money laundering network patterns
  • Multi-cloud risk diversification — 84% of infrastructure on AWS + Azure; reduces single-provider operational dependency
  • APRA and ACSC alignment — aligned to Australian Government Cyber Security Strategy; ACSC partnership for threat intelligence

  • Fusion Centre — integrated physical, cyber, and global operations; real-time incident detection and response; CrowdStrike as enterprise endpoint platform
